Approaches to Learning: A Re-Evaluation

Last updated on Feb 18, 2020 11 min read

Introduction

For my first blog post, I thought I would provide an outline of a work in progress paper that seeks to re-evaluate the work of Biggs, Kember, and Leung (2001). This previous work of Biggs, Kember, and Leung (2001) led to the creation of the Revised Two-Factor Study Process Questionnaire (R-SPQ-2F) that has been extensively used, predominately as a means of profiling students (i.e., categorising students as Deep or Surface learners). Using data acquired from an institutional survey containing the 20-item R-SPQ-2F (n = 1158), two proposed models of Biggs, Kember, and Leung (2001) were evaluated – a general summary is given below.

Proposed Models

The first model is presented in Figure 1 and shows four factors (Deep Motive, Deep Strategy, Surface Strategy, and Surface Motive), each of which is measured by five items. The following correlations are specified:

  • Between Deep Motive and Deep Strategy
  • Between Deep Motive and Surface Motive
  • Between Surfrace Strategy and Surface Motive

No correlations were specified between the errors of any of the 20 items

The original work of Biggs, Kember, and Leung (2001) reported a Comparative Fit Index (CFI) of .904 and Root Mean Square Residual (RMSR) of .058 for this first model. The latter does not fully meet the repeatedly touted rule of thumbs resulting from the simulation work of Hu and Bentler (1999) (i.e., CFI = .95, SRMR = .08).

Figure 1: Proposed Item Level Model of the R-SPQ-2F

Model two is presented in Figure 2 and is composed of two higher-order factors, each measured by two factors. The two lower-order factors were created through item parcelling and resulted in a marked improvement in model fit (CFI = .992, SRMR = .015; Biggs, Kember, and Leung 2001).

Figure 2: Higher Order Factor Model of the R-SPQ-2F

Obtaining a “better fitting” model through the process of item-parcelling is a contentious issue, particularly as it masks areas of ill-fit. Marsh et al. (2013) have previously stated that only when the conservative assumptions of Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) are supported (i.e., no cross-loadings or correlated errors) then item-parcelling may be appropriate as the factors are unidimensional.

Re-Analysis

Using the aforementioned collected data, I will demonstrate that both models (Figure 1 and 2) are not supported.

Model One

To explore Model One (Figure 1), the data will be analysed using CFA, following the specifications of Biggs, Kember, and Leung (2001).

biggs_model_one <- '

  deep_strategy =~ Q1 + Q5 + Q9 + Q13 + Q17
  
  deep_motive =~ Q2 + Q6 + Q10 + Q14 + Q18
  
  surface_strategy =~ Q3 + Q7 + Q11 + Q15 + Q19
  
  surface_motive =~ Q4 + Q8 + Q12 + Q16 + Q20
  
  deep_strategy ~~ deep_motive
  deep_strategy ~~ 0*surface_strategy
  deep_strategy ~~ 0*surface_motive
  
  deep_motive ~~ surface_motive
  deep_motive ~~ 0*surface_strategy
  
  surface_strategy ~~ surface_motive
'

biggs_model_one_cfa <-

Model One was found to not be positive definite and can be attributed to a model implied covariance value of -.008 between the Deep Motive and Surface Motive factors. The model can viewed as being misspecified and is therefore rejected.

Model Two

Three points can be made on the basis of the EFA results:

  • A four factor solution does not seem to be supported – the third factor has seven absolute values below .30
  • Unidimensionality of the purported four factors is not supported – some items have loadings on non-target factors that are not of a nominal value (e.g., item 11)
  • There may be a case for a two factor model (Deep and Surface Approaches) without the need to break these up into Strategy and Motive factors.

Conclusion

The results do not provide support for the original work of Biggs, Kember, and Leung (2001):

  • Model One (Figure 1) results in an inadmissible solution
  • Model Two (Figure 2) does not support the criteria of unidimensionality (Marsh et al. 2013) that would make item-parcelling an admissible approach

The work reiterates the importance of measurement and how this could potentially impact further research. And while the original models were not supported, there may be utility in exploring alternative factor models or approaches altogether (e.g., the characterisation of trace data).
